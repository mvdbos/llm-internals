Lesson 1: Tensors & Layers

The building blocks of an LLM — what tensors and layers actually are.

What's a tensor?

A tensor is a grid of numbers. You already know them by other names:

From a single number to a cube of numbers — they're all tensors
DimensionNameWhat it looks likeExample
0D Scalar A single number 42
1D Vector A row of numbers [0.2, 0.8, -0.5]
2D Matrix A table of numbers A spreadsheet grid
3D+ Tensor A cube (or more) of numbers A stack of spreadsheets

In practice, ML people call everything a tensor — scalars, vectors, matrices, all of it. The word just means "numbers flowing through the model."

Think of it like a factory conveyor belt. Each item on the belt is a tray with compartments holding numbers — that tray is a tensor. The model moves trays from station to station, and each station transforms the numbers in some way.

What's a layer?

A layer is one of those stations. It takes a tensor in, does math to it, and spits a (usually different) tensor out.

Input tensor  →  [ Layer: math operations ]  →  Output tensor

What math? Mostly two things:

  1. Multiply the input numbers by a set of stored numbers called weights
  2. Add some more stored numbers called biases

The weights are the "knowledge" of the layer — they're what the model learned during training. When you download an LLM, you're downloading billions of these weight numbers.

🧠 Key insight

The entire "intelligence" of an LLM lives in its weights. There's no database, no lookup table, no magic — just billions of carefully tuned numbers that, when multiplied and added together in the right sequence, produce coherent text.

How layers stack into an LLM

An LLM is a stack of layers. Like this:


Words → [Embedding layer] → [Layer 1] → [Layer 2] → ... → [Layer N] → [Output layer] → Next word
         (words→numbers)     (transformer blocks — the "thinking")      (numbers→word predictions)

Let's break each part down:

The three kinds of layers in a typical LLM
PartWhat it doesELI5
Embedding layer Converts each word into a tensor (a vector of ~4096 or ~8192 numbers) Turns "cat" into a fingerprint of numbers that captures what "cat" means
Transformer layers The bulk of the model. Each layer mixes information between words and transforms the representations. A 7B model might have 32 of these. 32 rounds of mixing information between words to build up context
Output layer Converts the final tensor back into a probability for each possible next word After all the processing: "the next word is probably 'sat'"

Why this matters for quantization

Every one of those layers stores its weights as numbers — floating-point numbers that take up 16 or 32 bits each. In a 7 billion parameter model:

  • At 16-bit (FP16): 7B × 2 bytes = 14 GB
  • At 4-bit (Q4): 7B × 0.5 bytes = 3.5 GB

Quantization compresses those weight numbers — from 16 bits down to 8 bits, 4 bits, or even lower. The tradeoff: fewer bits = less memory, but each weight is slightly fuzzier. Some layers care more about precision than others. That's what we'll explore next.
