Lesson 5: Choosing and Validating a Quant

Suppose two candidates both work with your runtime and fit in memory. One is smaller or faster; the other may preserve more quality. A suffix cannot settle the choice. Use the amount of validation that matches how important the decision is.

1. Start with hard constraints

  1. Keep only candidates compatible with the intended runtime and hardware.
  2. Keep only candidates that fit with required context and headroom.
  3. Shortlist two or three candidates rather than every available quant.

2. Compare the exact work you care about

Hold the checkpoint, task, runtime settings, prompts/tools, and environment steady. Measure task quality first, then interpret memory and speed with that quality. Report failures rather than quietly removing them.

Use a direct quality signal for the workload
WorkloadDirect quality signal
CodingVerified tests or task-specific checks
ClassificationAccuracy, F1, or equivalent measure
SummarizationFactuality and required-content criteria
Chat or writingDefined rubric or blinded preference
Agentic taskExternally verified completion

3. Match validation to the stakes

Proportional validation avoids both overconfidence and unnecessary research overhead
StakesAppropriate validation
Casual or reversible useRepresentative smoke tests and obvious failure checks
Important recurring workloadMatched tasks under each shortlisted candidate; repeat stochastic outputs
Public, expensive, or causal claimPreregistered paired/repeated protocol from the advanced reference

You do not need a research-grade experiment for every local choice. You do need enough evidence for the risk of being wrong.

4. Record a supported choice

Selected model/checkpoint: [...]
Hardware and usable memory: [...]
Compatible runtime: [...]
Required context/headroom: [...]
Shortlisted candidates: A / B / C
Validation level: smoke test / matched tasks / controlled study
Primary quality result: [...]
Memory and speed result: [...]
Choice: [...]
Remaining uncertainty: [...]

Choose the supported trade-off, not a universal winner. The planned controlled case study will demonstrate the same method with evidence when its gate is met; you can use this method now. Next action: pick two or three compatible candidates, run the validation level that matches your stakes, then complete this record before deciding.

Quiz: make the practical choice

1. Why are compatibility and fit hard constraints?

2. What should guide a choice before speed?

3. When are matched repeated tasks appropriate?

4. Why cannot unmatched tasks isolate a quantization effect?
